Key Topics in Data Communication

Data Transmission Modes

Simplex, Half-Duplex, and Full-Duplex modes define how data flows between devices in a communication channel.

Analog vs Digital Signals

Analog signals are continuous waves while digital signals use discrete binary values (0s and 1s) for data representation.

Network Topologies

Bus, Star, Ring, Mesh, and Hybrid topologies describe the physical and logical layout of a network.

Transmission Media

Guided media (coaxial, fiber optic, twisted pair) and unguided media (radio waves, microwaves, infrared).

Error Detection & Correction

Parity check, CRC, and Hamming codes are used to detect and correct errors in transmitted data.

OSI Model

The 7-layer OSI model standardizes communication functions: Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application.

Summary of Data Communication Studies

Below is a summary of key concepts studied in Data Communication, with a focus on Satellite Communication.

Satellite Architecture

A satellite communication system has three main segments: the Space Segment (the satellite in orbit), the Ground Segment (earth stations and dish panels), and the Control Segment (monitoring and control systems). Data travels via uplink (earth to satellite) and downlink (satellite to earth) using radio waves boosted by transponders.

Types of Satellite Orbits

GEO (Geostationary Earth Orbit) — stays fixed above earth at 35,786 km, good for TV and internet but causes latency. LEO (Low Earth Orbit) — flies closer to earth, faster speed, less delay. Used by Starlink. Requires many satellites to cover the whole earth. MEO (Medium Earth Orbit) — sits between GEO and LEO, used for GPS systems.

High-Throughput Satellites (HTS)

HTS uses spot beam technology (also called "Satellite Zooming") — instead of one wide beam, it sends multiple narrow focused beams to smaller areas. This allows frequency reuse, stronger signals, and faster internet. Key examples include ViaSat-1 (140 Gbit/s capacity), KA-SAT with 82 spot beams, and SpaceX Starlink's LEO constellation.

Challenges in Satellite Communication

Major challenges include: Latency (signal delay — GEO satellites have ~600ms delay), Attenuation (signal weakening due to rain, clouds, dust), Space Congestion (300,000+ collision-avoidance maneuvers by Starlink in 2025), High Cost (expensive to build and launch), and Security Risks (data interception by hackers).

High-Resolution Data Acquisition

Satellites capture detailed images of earth using optical sensors (like cameras) and SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar) which works through clouds and at night. Applications include: precision agriculture, disaster management, military surveillance, city planning, and monitoring illegal activities like galamsey in Ghana. NASA's Landsat 8 captures images with 30cm resolution.

Future of Satellite Communication

The future includes: Laser Inter-Satellite Links (ISLs) — satellites communicating via lasers in space, Direct-to-Cell (D2C) — connecting phones directly to satellites without dish panels, Edge Computing in Orbit — AI-powered satellites that analyze data in space before sending to earth, and Optical Communication — 10-100x faster data speeds using laser links.

The Telegraph – A Historic Communication Device

The telegraph was one of the earliest forms of electrical long-distance communication, invented in the 1830s–1840s.

History

Invented by Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail in the 1830s. The first telegraph message was sent in 1844.

Morse Code

Messages were encoded as dots and dashes representing letters and numbers, transmitted as electrical pulses.

Impact

The telegraph revolutionized long-distance communication and laid the foundation for modern networking.
